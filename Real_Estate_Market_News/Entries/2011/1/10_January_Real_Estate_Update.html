January Real Estate Update

 

What’s in store for 2011?

The chief economist for the National Association of Realtors has announced that in 2011 consumers can, “Expect higher mortgage rates, relatively flat home prices and a rebound in home sales.”  Furthermore, a recovery would lead to a drop in foreclosures, which have run at about 2 million a year the past two years versus 400,000 a year in previous periods.

Affordability is also in the market's favor. The monthly mortgage payment on the median-priced metro-Denver home is $257 less than it was in the second quarter of 2005.  Tighter underwriting standards have resulted in fewer new loans going bad, making 2010 the best "vintage" for mortgages ever at the Federal Housing Administration, according the National Association of Realtors.  That strong performance might encourage lenders to loosen up a little more and qualify potential buyers now blocked out of the market.  The forecast calls for mortgage rates, which briefly approached 4 percent, to return to 5 percent next year and 5.9 percent in 2012 as inflationary pressures build in the economy.  Bottom line, despite a drop in home sales in December, most market analysts are expecting good things to come in the months ahead.


2011: New Colorado Contracts

If you’ve ever bought or sold real estate in Colorado, starting January 1st, expect to see a totally different real estate contract.  In an attempt to simply contracts for consumers, the Colorado Real Estate Commission is introducing contracts geared towards specific property types.  Instead of wondering why you’re checking a box indicating you don’t have any growing  crops in your condominium, the five new contracts will be much better suited to your specific property type.


Mortgages:

According to a new Lending Tree survey, while a overwhelming 96 percent of American consumers compare prices when shopping for almost anything, nearly 40 percent obtain just one home loan quote.  Though it is a decision that will affect them for the next 15-30 years, nearly three-quarters (72 percent) of homeowners spent the equivalent of a full working day or less shopping for their home loan.  With less than one percent affecting your monthly payment by an average $100, consider spending the time to investigating a wide array of choices.  Not all loans are created equal!
